2. Revenue

Enter what you sell, at what price, and how many per month. Use your most common product or service, or a weighted average if you offer several.

Revenue is not profit

Revenue is the total amount customers pay you — before any costs, and excluding GST. A $550 invoice (inc. GST) contributes $500 to revenue.

Not sure what to charge? Search what competitors in your area charge, or check industry price guides on business.gov.au.

3. Cost of Goods Sold (direct costs)

List every cost that is directly tied to producing what you sell. These costs scale with volume — if you sell nothing, these are zero.

Direct cost test

Would this cost increase if you sold one more unit? If yes, it is a direct cost. If it stays the same regardless of sales, it belongs in Section 4.

Not sure? The Benchmark Helper shows typical cost of sales percentages for your business type. Multiply your revenue by that percentage to estimate.

4. Operating expenses (fixed costs)

List every recurring cost that is not directly tied to production. These are paid whether or not you make a single sale. Be thorough — underestimating fixed costs is one of the most common errors in student P&Ls.

Don't forget yourself

Include your own minimum pay as an operating expense. If you leave it out, your net profit will be overstated and your business will appear more viable than it is.

Need wage estimates? Check minimum rates at fairwork.gov.au or typical salaries at seek.com.au. For rent, search commercial leases for your suburb at realestate.com.au/commercial.
